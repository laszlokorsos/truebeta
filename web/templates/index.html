Understanding Beta

Beta is the single most important number in portfolio risk management. Here's why the way you estimate it matters.

β

What is Beta?

Beta (β) measures how much a stock moves relative to the overall market. A beta of 1.5 means the stock tends to move 1.5% for every 1% move in the S&P 500. It's the cornerstone of modern portfolio theory, risk management, and hedging — used by every institutional investor to size positions and manage exposure.

OLS

The Problem with Fixed Beta

Traditional beta is estimated using a fixed window of historical data — typically 1 year of daily returns regressed against the market. This assumes beta is constant within that window. But it's not. Market conditions, business fundamentals, leverage changes, and investor sentiment cause beta to shift continuously. A single number hides the whole story.

KF

Dynamic Beta via Kalman Filter

The Kalman filter models beta as a continuously evolving state variable rather than a fixed parameter. At each time step, it optimally combines its prediction (based on yesterday's estimate) with the new observation (today's returns), producing a smooth, adaptive beta estimate along with a measure of uncertainty.

Why Kalman Filtering?

The Kalman filter is an optimal recursive estimator used to navigate spacecraft, track missiles, and now — estimate your stock's true market sensitivity.

Rolling OLS Limitations

  • ×
    Arbitrary window choice

    60 days? 252 days? The answer changes the result dramatically, and there's no principled way to choose.

  • ×
    Lagging

    The rolling window reacts slowly to regime changes. By the time it adjusts, the opportunity is gone.

  • ×
    Abrupt jumps

    As extreme observations enter or leave the window, beta can jump discontinuously, creating false signals.

  • ×
    Equal weighting

    All observations in the window get equal weight, whether from yesterday or 11 months ago.

Kalman Filter Advantages

  • ✓
    No window to choose

    The filter automatically determines how much to weight new vs. old data based on the signal-to-noise ratio.

  • ✓
    Adapts in real time

    Beta updates at every observation, so regime changes are captured immediately.

  • ✓
    Smooth transitions

    The state-space framework produces smooth beta paths without artificial jumps.

  • ✓
    Uncertainty quantification

    The filter provides confidence intervals around beta, so you know how certain the estimate is.

The State-Space Model

We model stock returns using the classic market model, but allow beta to evolve over time.

Observation Equation

$$(r_{i,t} - r_{f,t}) = \alpha_t + \beta_t \cdot (r_{m,t} - r_{f,t}) + \varepsilon_t, \quad \varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R)$$

Excess stock return = alpha + beta $\times$ excess market return + noise

State Equation

$$\beta_t = \beta_{t-1} + w_t, \quad w_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, Q)$$

Beta follows a random walk — it drifts gradually over time

Kalman Update

$$K_t = \frac{P_{t|t-1} \, H_t^\top}{H_t \, P_{t|t-1} \, H_t^\top + R}$$
$$\hat{x}_{t|t} = \hat{x}_{t|t-1} + K_t \left( r_{i,t} - H_t \, \hat{x}_{t|t-1} \right)$$

The Kalman gain $K_t$ balances how much to trust the new observation vs. the model prediction. When uncertainty is high, the filter leans on the data; when data is noisy, it trusts the model.
